#HCI Challenge 1: City Access(ibility)

Palas Mall Accessibility Improvements

Context of use

Location

We chose Palas Mall for our project due to its central location in Iasi and its popularity among diverse visitors. As the largest mall in the city, it offers us valuable insights into real-world usability challenges. By addressing these issues, we can make a meaningful impact on the local community, creating a more inclusive and enjoyable environment for all visitors.

Layout

The layout of the shopping mall consists of multiple floors connected by escalators, elevators, or stairs. Stores are typically arranged in clusters, with anchor stores located at opposite ends of the mall. There are also common areas for dining, entertainment, and relaxation, such as food courts, cinema, and seating areas.

Environment

Palas Mall is usually a busy and vibrant environment, with a lot of visual and auditory stimuli. It can be noisy and crowded, especially during peak hours or holidays. Lighting and temperature are carefully controlled to create a comfortable and inviting atmosphere. There are also various amenities such as restrooms, ATMs, and information desks to assist visitors.

Users

The users of the shopping mall are diverse and can include people of all ages, genders, and cultural backgrounds. They visit the mall for different reasons, such as shopping, dining, entertainment, or socializing. Some visitors are frequent shoppers who know their way around, while others may be first-time visitors who are unfamiliar with the layout and amenities.

Goals and Tasks

The goals and tasks of mall visitors can vary widely depending on their reasons for visiting. Shoppers may be looking for specific items or simply browsing for inspiration. Diners may be looking for a quick bite or a leisurely meal. Entertainment seekers may be looking for a specific movie or game, or just something fun to do. Visitors may also have different needs and preferences, such as accessibility requirements, language barriers, or cultural sensitivities.

Metaphors/idioms, affordances and visual variables

Metaphors/idioms, affordances, and visual variables are all important design elements that can greatly enhance the usability and user experience of a shopping mall. Here are some examples of how these design elements could be used:

Metaphors/Idioms

  • A shopping cart metaphor could be used in the mall's website to make it easier for users to add items to their cart and checkout.
  • The idiom "swipe left" could be used to communicate to users that they can swipe left to see more product details.
  • The idiom "tap to select" could be used to communicate to users that they can tap on a product to select it.

Affordances

  • The affordance of a push/pull handle could be used on doors to communicate how to open them.
  • The affordance of a tactile surface on a door handle could communicate to users that they should pull the door to open it.
  • The affordance of a button on an elevator could be used to communicate how to call the elevator.

Visual variables

  • A specific color scheme or logo could be used to distinguish between different types of stores or departments in a mall map.
  • The size and placement of store signs could be used to communicate their relative importance and location within the mall.
  • The use of icons and symbols could be used to create a visual association between a store and its products or services.

Usability problems observed and potential improvements:

  • Inadequate signage for restrooms, exits, and stores:
    Improvement: Install larger signs with high-contrast colors, braille, and universally recognized symbols to help all visitors navigate more easily.
  • Limited seating areas, especially near high-traffic locations:
    Improvement: Add comfortable seating areas throughout the mall, with a focus on high-traffic locations and rest areas. Ensure these seating spaces have wheelchair accessible seating as well.
  • Touchscreen directory maps with small fonts and poor contrast:
    Improvement: Redesign the touchscreen directories to include larger fonts, high-contrast colors, and simplified graphics. Add auditory feedback to make the interface more accessible for visually impaired users.
  • Narrow aisles and inaccessible store entrances for wheelchair users:
    Improvement: Work with store owners to widen aisles and modify store entrances to accommodate wheelchair users. Implement accessible ramps and automatic doors where necessary.
  • No audible guidance for visually impaired visitors:
    Improvement: Introduce an audio-guidance system using strategically placed speakers, Bluetooth beacons, or a dedicated mobile app to provide directions and information for visually impaired visitors.

Additional software improvements:

  • Mobile app for personalized mall navigation:
    Develop a mobile app that offers personalized navigation, including accessible routes and turn-by-turn directions for all visitors, including those with special needs. The app can also provide real-time information on store promotions and events.
  • Indoor positioning system (IPS):
    Implement an indoor positioning system using Wi-Fi, Bluetooth, or other technologies to help users locate themselves within the mall, enhancing their navigation experience.
  • Augmented reality (AR) support:
    Integrate augmented reality features into the mobile app to provide users with visual overlays that highlight important information, such as store names, accessible entrances, and other points of interest.
  • Conversational UI agent:
    Incorporate a conversational UI agent, such as a chatbot, into the mobile app to provide users with an alternative way to access information and ask questions, enhancing the overall user experience.

Empirical laws, affordances, and flow in the context of the improved solutions:

  • Fitts' Law
    Definition: States that the time it takes to move to a target area is a function of the distance to the target and the size of the target.
    Usage: By making interactive elements, such as touchscreen directories and mobile app interfaces, more accessible and easier to use, visitors can interact with them more efficiently.
  • Hick' Law
    Definition: States that the time it takes to make a decision is a function of the number of options available.
    Usage: Simplified navigation and reduced choices on the touchscreen directory and mobile app help users make decisions faster, resulting in a more streamlined experience.
  • Steering' Law
    Definition: States that the time it takes to move a cursor to a target is a function of the angle between the starting position and the target, as well as the distance to the target.
    Usage: Wider aisles, accessible store entrances, and improved navigation minimize the need for complex, precision-based movements, enabling users to move more easily through the mall.
  • Affordances
    Clear visual cues, such as high-contrast signage and accessible route indicators, help users better understand the environment and navigate more effectively.
  • Flow
    The combination of improved navigation, better accessibility, and enhanced usability create a more enjoyable and efficient experience for all users, including those with special needs. As a result, visitors will be more likely to have a positive experience and return to the shopping mall in the future.
